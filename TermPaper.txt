Abstract
Introduction
The hypersonic flight regime imposes strict demands on the combustion process. At flight Mach numbers greater than five, the core flow residence time in the combustor is on the millisecond scale, while temperatures and heat fluxes severely constrain material choice. Under these conditions, combustion is no longer solely a chemical process, but a coupled aerothermodynamic process in which mixing, reaction rates, and compressible flow effects all determine thermal efficiency. The central question is not simply how to sustain combustion, but how to do so while maintaining high thermodynamic efficiency across the flight envelope. 
Conventional scramjet combustors address this problem using supersonic deflagration, extending constant-pressure heat addition to the supersonic regime. While this approach is sufficient for steady-state operation and relatively stable flowfields, it inherits the fundamental limitations of the Brayton cycle. Constant-pressure heat addition generates significant entropy, which directly reduces the thermal efficiency. As a result, even idealized scramjet cycles are bounded in achievable thermal efficiency, as shown in the closed cycle analysis in section 4.2.1 of [1.]
Alternative concepts seek to overcome this limitation through pressure-gain combustion, most often detonation. In these systems, heat release occurs closer to constant-volume conditions, increasing the pressure rise across the combustor and reducing entropy generation. Pulse detonation engines (PDEs) achieve this process intermittently through repeated filling and detonation of a chamber, while rotating detonation engines (RDEs) attempt to sustain one or more continuously propagating detonation waves in an annular geometry. Although these approaches offer a higher theoretical efficiency ceiling, strong unsteadiness, shock-induced losses, and substantial integration challenges are inherent to their practical implementation [2.]
A consistent evaluation of these architectures requires a framework that links combustion mode to efficiency and loss mechanisms. In the hypersonic regime, all combustor concepts are constrained by finite-rate mixing, limited residence time, total pressure losses, and extreme thermal loads that necessitate active cooling and restrict fuel selection. This paper examines conventional scramjet combustors, PDEs, RDEs, and emerging hybrid concepts within this framework, with the objective of identifying the dominant mechanisms that govern achievable efficiency and the technological bottlenecks that limit their realization.
Thermodynamic and Physical Basis for High-Efficiency Combustion (≈1 page)
Thermal efficiency provides the primary link between combustor design and propulsion system performance. For airbreathing engines, overall efficiency is the product of thermal and propulsive efficiency, with the thermal component governed by the thermodynamic cycle in the engine. The present work focuses on thermal efficiency as the relevant metric for comparing combustor architectures, since it reflects how effectively chemical energy is converted into useful work. Differences between combustor concepts can be understood in terms of the thermodynamic path and associated entropy generation.
The Brayton cycle serves as the baseline for conventional airbreathing propulsion systems, including ramjets and scramjets. In this cycle, heat addition occurs at quasi-constant pressure following isentropic compression. The resulting thermal efficiency depends primarily on overall pressure ratio, which is either provided or significantly augmented by ram compression at hypersonic speeds. This advantage is somewhat offset by pressure losses internal to the cycle. Any entropy generation inside the combustor, whether due to mixing, viscous effects, or shocks, directly reduces total pressure and degrades the fraction of energy converted to thrust. As a result, even idealized ram cycles are constrained by the inherent inefficiency of constant-pressure heat addition.
The Humphrey cycle provides a conceptual upper limit for pressure-gain combustion. In this idealization, heat addition occurs at a constant volume, producing a pressure rise across the combustor rather than a loss. For a given operating point, this results in lower entropy generation and higher thermal efficiency than the Brayton cycle. The difference is a fundamental result of the thermodynamic path; Increasing pressure during heat addition allows a greater portion of the fuel’s chemical energy to be converted into work. Detonation based engines such as PDEs and RDEs approximate this cycle, and their development is motivated by this thermodynamic advantage [Cite.]
The difference between these cycles is rooted in the underlying combustion mode. Deflagration is a subsonic process governed by turbulent flame propagation which requires thorough mixing, in traditional combustors it results in approximately constant pressure heat release. Detonation, by contrast, is a supersonic phenomenon in which a shock front compresses the reactants to ignition components in a self-sustaining wave. The resulting rapid pressure rise provides the physical mechanism for pressure gain combustion. While this process offers clear thermodynamic benefits, it also introduces strong unsteadiness and shock-induced losses that complicate practical implementation.
Real combustors deviate from both ideal cycles due to irreversibilities. Finite rate chemistry, incomplete mixing, and shockwaves all generate entropy. In compressible flow, these effects primarily result in total pressure loss, which directly reduces engine performance. Additional losses arise in unsteady systems (PDEs and RDEs) including inefficiencies with repeated filling and evacuation, as well as losses tied to detonation initiation. These factors limit how closely any practical system can mimic the ideal Humphrey cycle.
The hypersonic flight regime imposes further constraints that compound these losses. Combustor residence time is on the order of milliseconds, making mixing the limiting factor for combustion. High total temperatures promote dissociation. At the same time, large dynamic pressures make even small total pressure losses significant. These conditions constrain allowable heat addition and complicate practical applications for both deflagration and detonation-based approaches.
These considerations define the evaluation framework used in the rest of this paper. Each combustor concept is assessed based on how closely it approaches the ideal cycle while managing losses associated with mixing, unsteadiness, finite residence time, and total pressure degradation. The Brayton cycle serves as the baseline for conventional operation, while the Humphrey cycle defines the upper limit of efficiency. The performance of each architecture is determined by how it balances efficiency against practical constraints.
Traditional Scramjet Combustors 
The scramjet extends the Brayton cycle into the hypersonic regime by sustaining combustion in a supersonic flow. Unlike turbojets, which rely on mechanical compression, and ramjets, which decelerate the flow to subsonic conditions prior to combustion, scramjets maintain supersonic conditions throughout the combustor to avoid the large total pressure losses associated with normal shocks. Compression is instead achieved through a series of oblique shocks in the inlet, producing a high-temperature, high-pressure flow suitable for combustion without full stagnation. As a result, the scramjet operates under a modified Brayton cycle with approximately constant-pressure heat addition, but with significantly tighter constraints on mixing, reaction time, and total pressure preservation due to the high-speed flow environment.
The scramjet flowpath consists of an inlet, isolator, combustor, and nozzle. In the lower hypersonic regime, roughly up to Mach 8, scramjets operate in a dual-mode configuration. In this regime, the inlet compresses the oncoming flow through a series of oblique shocks, delivering supersonic air to the combustor. Heat release from combustion produces a pressure rise that can induce boundary layer separation along the combustor walls. This results in a series of compression shocks known as a shock train, which can propagate upstream and disturb inlet flow conditions. To prevent the shock train from disrupting inlet operation, the isolator section is added as a buffer to stabilize the flowfield. Depending on operating conditions, the combined effects of diffusion in the isolator and heat addition in the combustor can force the core flow to subsonic speeds, requiring reacceleration through a thermal throat. At higher Mach numbers, the increased kinetic energy of the flow reduces the relative impact of heat release, suppressing separation and allowing the combustor to operate fully supersonic. This dual-mode behavior highlights the sensitivity of the scramjet propulsion system to operating condition and the coupling between hypersonic vehicle and propulsion system.
Scramjets rely on deflagration as their combustion mechanism. Deflagration in a supersonic flow presents serious challenges with flameholding, as the flow is moving faster than the flame propagation speed. Due to this, scramjets must employ complex flameholding devices such as cavities or recirculation zones, which come with associated efficiency penalties. 
Under Rayleigh flow, heat addition drives the supersonic flow toward a thermally choked state at Mach 1, limiting the allowable total temperature rise and, in turn, the maximum fuel-air ratio that the combustor can sustain. This constraint is a direct source of irreversibility, the process of constant-pressure heat addition generates entropy at a rate proportional to local Mach number, meaning that the scramjet’s combustion efficiency is actively penalized as it travels further into the hypersonic regime [Anderson, Modern Compressible Flow.] In a vehicle operating at the upper end of a scramjet’s Mach envelope, these losses can make up a significant fraction of the total pressure recovered by the inlet [Heiser & Pratt.] This creates tension at the core of scramjet design, where the same high-velocity flow that makes airbreathing propulsion viable at hypersonic speeds is the mechanism by which combustion-driven pressure losses increase. It is possible to offset Rayleigh losses through geometric combustor design, but additional wave drag & separation risk are the associated cost, and these patches do not address the fundamental entropy generation problem. 
Thermal management is a primary design component in scramjet combustors due to the extreme heat fluxes generated by high total temperatures and sustained combustion. At the highest efficiency operating conditions, wall temperatures approach or exceed material limits, necessitating active cooling strategies to maintain structural integrity. Regenerative cooling, in which the fuel is circulated through chambers in the combustor walls prior to injection is one commonly employed method. This approach restricts fuel selection, since the fuel must possess sufficient heat capacity and chemical stability to serve as an effective coolant. Hydrogen fuel provides high cooling capacity and rapid ignition characteristics, but poses storage and integration challenges, not to mention the difficulty in establishing infrastructure. Hydrocarbon fuels leverage existing infrastructure, are easier to store, and provide a high volumetric energy density, but carry a much lower specific energy and exhibit more complex combustion chemistry.  
The operational envelope of scramjet engines is similarly constrained by chemical & aerothermodynamic considerations. Efficient operation is typically limited to a relatively narrow range of flight Mach numbers where inlet compression provides sufficient temperature and pressure to sustain ignition without incurring excessive total pressure loss. At lower speeds, the incoming flow lacks the thermal energy required for sustained combustion, making scramjets infeasible without auxiliary propulsion systems. At higher Mach numbers, although compression increases, the corresponding rise in temperature can lead to dissociation and reduced combustion efficiency, while encroaching on structural and thermal limits. Within their viable range, scramjets offer stable steady-state operation relative to unsteady detonation systems. However, this stability comes at the expense of an inherent efficiency ceiling imposed by the Brayton cycle.
Taken together, these characteristics pose scramjets as the most mature and practically demonstrated technology for hypersonic airbreathing combustion. They provide reliable operation within a narrow flight envelope and have been validated in multiple experimental programs. However, their performance remains fundamentally constrained by the limits of the Brayton cycle and the unavoidable losses associated with mixing and total pressure loss. These limitations motivate the exploration of alternative combustion strategies, primarily pressure-gain concepts like detonation engines, which offer inbuilt efficiency boosts at a given operating condition.
PDEs
The concept of pulsed combustion dates to the late nineteenth century, with its first large-scale aerospace application coming during World War II in the pulsejet-powered V-1 flying bomb. PDEs extend this concept into the detonation regime, representing a class of pressure-gain combustors that seek to provide thermodynamic efficiency gains through unsteady detonation rather than steady deflagration. In a PDE, a fuel-air mixture is periodically detonated, producing a shock front that propagates through the chamber and generates a rapid non-uniform pressure rise. This process more closely approximates constant-volume heat addition than the approximately constant-pressure combustion of conventional scramjets, reducing entropy generation and increasing theoretical cycle efficiency. The engine operates in a cycle of intake, ignition, detonation, and expansion, with thrust produced from the high-pressure exhaust following each cycle. While the underlying concept is straightforward, the requirement to reliably initiate detonation in a high-speed flow introduces significant unsteadiness and imposes constraints on mixing, timing, and structural design that separate PDEs from both traditional airbreathing engines and other detonation engines.
PDE development is motivated by the thermodynamic advantage of detonation-based cycles. As discussed in section Two, this advantage arises from reduced entropy generation associated with approximately constant-volume heat addition. In contrast to deflagration-based systems, where heat addition occurs at approximately constant pressure conditions, the detonation process produces a rapid, nonuniform pressure rise within the combustor. The resulting cycle is referred to as the Humphrey cycle, which exhibits higher thermal efficiency than the Brayton cycle for a given operating point. The increase in efficiency is a consequence of the different thermodynamic path, by increasing pressure during heat addition, a larger fraction of the fuel’s latent chemical energy is converted into useful work. This efficiency gain provides the primary impetus for PDE research, despite the significant integration challenges. 
The remaining development challenges for PDEs are a direct consequence of the unsteady detonation process at the core of their thermodynamic advantage. Reliable detonation initiation remains a primary obstacle. Practical systems must repeatedly transition from a deflagration process to fully developed detonation within very short timescales, which typically requires long deflagration-to-detonation transition (DDT) regions or supplementary ignition systems. Both approaches impose penalties in system size, complexity, and weight. Even when suitable initiators are employed, variability in ignition and wave propagation from cycle to cycle can degrade performance and complicate control.
Mixture preparation presents additional challenges. Detonation requires a relatively narrow envelope of fuel-air conditions, so the filling process must produce a sufficiently homogeneous and reactive mixture in the brief window between detonations. This requirement becomes increasingly difficult as operating frequency rises, since incomplete mixing can lead to failed detonations or reduced wave strength. These failures are directly linked to thrust production, meaning that the average thrust of a poorly conditioned PDE can vary unpredictably, leading to undesirable flight characteristics. 
The structural and fluid dynamic conditions within the detonation chamber are also severe. The detonation wave produces steep pressure and temperature gradients, followed by rapid blowdown, subjecting the chamber to cyclic thermal and mechanical loading. This raises concerns regarding fatigue, sealing, and the transmission of unsteady thrust loads to the airframe. Downstream, nozzle integration is complicated by the inherently unsteady exhaust. Maintaining choked flow at the throat in the presence of oscillatory back pressure is challenging, and poorly matched nozzle geometries can reflect pressure waves back into the chamber, disrupting subsequent cycles and, in extreme cases, inducing backfiring. These effects introduce additional losses that offset the theoretical efficiency gains of the detonation cycle. 
These challenges limit the practical operating region of PDE-based propulsion. The need for discrete filling and detonation cycles constrains operating frequency and the smoothness of the thrust curve, while mixing requirements become increasingly constraining at higher flight speeds. As a result, PDE concepts are generally limited to the mid-supersonic regime, with performance degrading as conditions approach the hypersonic regime at Mach 5. For this reason, PDEs are often proposed as components in a combined cycle, where they serve as a transition between lower speeds and alternative combustion modes at higher Mach numbers. 
These limitations motivate the development of rotating detonation engines, which seek to retain the thermodynamic advantages of detonation while eliminating the inherently intermittent nature of the PDE cycle.
Rotating Detonation Engines (≈2 pages)
RDEs are a refinement of detonation-based combustion that seeks to approach the thermodynamic limits of the Humphrey cycle by approximating a steady flow configuration. Unlike PDEs, which rely on intermittent filling and detonation cycles, RDEs sustain one or more continuously propagating detonation waves inside the combustion chamber, operating at frequencies in the kilohertz range compared to the decahertz zone of PDEs [Braun.] This continuous mode of operation eliminates the need for discrete purge and refill processes, leading to improved thrust smoothness. At the same time, maintaining the cycle imposes much more strenuous timing and wave control requirements alongside the traditional ignition and unsteadiness issues. This is what sets rotating detonation engines apart from both conventional deflagration combustors and intermittent detonation systems.
In an RDE, reactants are continuously injected through an injector plate at the base of an annular combustion chamber, where one or more detonation waves propagate azimuthally at velocities on the order of kilometers per second. As each wave passes a given location, it encounters a layer of fresh reactants that have accumulated since the previous passage and initiates detonation, after which the products expand axially through the exhaust. This continuous, rotating process eliminates the discrete sequence that defines PDE operation. Because each fluid element experiences rapid compression and heat addition as the wave passes, the process approximates constant-volume combustion and more closely follows the Humphrey cycle than conventional deflagration-based systems. The canonical geometry consists of an annular chamber, an upstream injector plate that meters and distributes the reactants, and a downstream expansion section. In this configuration, the detonation wave itself provides a portion of the compression, reducing reliance on upstream precompression, although not eliminating it entirely. The interaction between the injected flow and the propagating detonation front is central to engine operation, as injector coupling governs mixture replenishment, wave stability, and the overall performance of the device.
The rotating detonation engine is relatively flexible in the world of airbreathing hypersonic propulsion, Zhang et al. have demonstrated the feasibility of an RDE concept at a Mach range of 2 to 6, and Beijing Power Machinery Institute alleges that their continuous RDE can operate up to Mach 7. As the Mach number approaches the limit, the inlet temperature increases, risking autoignition of the fuel-air mixture, and the backpressure resultant from the detonation begins to impinge on the fuel injection process. RDEs exhibit reduced sensitivity to mass flow rate compared to PDEs due to their continuous operation and provision for multiple detonation waves propagating concurrently. 
The stability of an RDE is governed by the wave dynamics within the combustor, which can be characterized by the number of waves and the flight Mach number. For a given geometry and operating point, the system settles at an equilibrium point, with multiple waves propagating at near-constant azimuthal velocity and spacing. The specific conditions reflect a balance between injection, energy release, and exhaust conditions, where each wave must encounter a sufficiently replenished mixture to sustain detonation. Deviations from this balance lead to negative wave interactions, including merging, splitting, or annihilation, disrupting the flow. Stable operation corresponds to a regime in which the wave number and propagation speed remain constant over time, indicating that the dynamics are matched. These dynamics directly tie to both controllability and operability, as fluctuations in wave structure manifest as unsteady pressure fields and thrust oscillations, which produce negative results for the flight vehicle. 
The principal technological challenges of RDEs arise directly from sustaining high-frequency, continuous detonation within a confined geometry. Detonation initiation and startup sequencing are nontrivial, as the engine must transition from a deflagrative state to a stable rotating wave pattern without inducing destructive transients or falling into instability. Once established, the detonation front imposes large, oscillatory backpressure on the injector, making injector–wave coupling a central constraint. The injection system must deliver a properly conditioned mixture while resisting flow reversal, choking, or phase mismatch with the passing waves, all of which can degrade stability or extinguish the detonation. These requirements are compounded by the need for rapid mixing, since the continuously replenished reactant layer must reach detonable conditions within extremely short timescales occasionally involving supersonic injection. At the same time, the combustor is subjected to severe thermal and mechanical loading due to the high-frequency passage of detonation waves, raising concerns related to material limits, fatigue, and cooling effectiveness. Downstream integration further complicates the system, as the unsteady exhaust must be expanded through a nozzle that can tolerate oscillatory pressure fields without incurring excessive losses or reflecting disturbances back into the chamber. Collectively, these challenges are not independent design issues but coupled consequences of the continuous detonation process, and they define the primary barriers to achieving stable, efficient RDE operation in practical propulsion systems.
RDE development has advanced from hypothetical ‘paper engines’ to hardware validation, although the technology remains well short of flight readiness. NASA Marshall Space Flight Center completed a sustained hotfire test of a full-scale RDE combustor in 2023, achieving 251 seconds of continuous operation [NASA,] the most significant endurance benchmark to date. Industry has demonstrated results as well; Pratt & Whitney tested a demonstrator in early 2025 as did the aforementioned BPMI. According to NASA, the remaining technological gaps are primarily integration related, rather than the combustion physics themselves, suggesting the field has moved past proof-of-concept. Airbreathing RDE configurations lag behind rocket variants in maturity level, and no sustained thrust measurement under realistic hypersonic conditions has been published as of this writing.
RDEs address the primary limitation of PDEs by implementing a steady flow condition under the pressure-gain architecture, keeping the thermodynamic benefits while improving thrust continuity and operating frequency. Relative to conventional scramjets, RDEs offer the potential for higher cycle efficiency through the pressure-gain heat addition process, but do so at the price of introducing strong internal unsteadiness and tighter operating tolerances. In particular, stability of the rotating wave structure and its interaction with the injection system do not present in traditional deflagration engines. As a result, while RDEs resolve some of the operability limitations, they come with their own unique set of fluid dynamic challenges. These tradeoffs highlight that no current architecture fully overcomes the fundamental constraints of hypersonic combustion, and point towards hybrid approaches. 
Emerging and Hybrid Concepts 
Emerging combustor concepts largely seek to close the gap between the thermodynamic potential of pressure-gain combustion and the practical limitations identified in existing methods. Rather than introducing entirely new cycle concepts, most recent efforts attempt to combine the positives of multiple combustion methods or to distribute heat losses in a way that mitigates mixing or unsteadiness degradation. The result is a set of hybrid architecture that attempts to improve operability and more effectively integrate with the flight vehicle.
At the system level, combined cycle propulsion remains the most popular approach to developing efficient operation across the flight envelope. Turbine based combined cycle (TBCC) and rocket based combined cycle (RBCC) engines employ multiple propulsion modes, transitioning between modes to maximize efficiency at a given flight condition. In these architectures, conventional turbomachinery or rocket operation supports low speed operation, while ramjet or scramjet combustion becomes dominant at higher Mach numbers. A famous, early example of combined cycle operation is the SR-71 Blackbird, whose Pratt & Whitney J58 turbojet engine functioned as a ramjet to speed the Blackbird in excess of Mach 3.2 [Cite.] The relevance to high-efficiency combustion lies in how these systems manage heat addition across regimes where neither pure deflagration nor detonation-based operation is optimal. In particular, the limitations of PDEs at higher Mach numbers and the poor low-speed performance of scramjets motivate architectures in which different combustion modes are used sequentially rather than forced to operate outside their effective range. However, these systems introduce significant integration challenges, including mode transition stability, inlet combustor coupling, and overall system weight.
In the combustor specifically, hybrid concepts attempt to capture some of the efficiency benefits of pressure-gain combustion while avoiding the strong unsteadiness associated with fully intermittent detonation cycles. These approaches include staged combustors in which localized regions of high reactivity support detonation processes embedded in otherwise continuous flow. The intent is to increase pressure during heat addition without global detonation. In practice, these configurations face competing requirements: maintaining sufficient stability for steady state operation while achieving significant pressure gain. As a result, they often realize only a portion of the theoretical efficiency improvement, but offer much improved controllability relative to PDEs. 
In parallel, several enabling technologies have emerged that support both conventional and detonation-based combustors. High-speed mixing strategies, including enhanced fuel injection schemes and geometric features that promote rapid shear-layer growth aim to mitigate the mixing constraint of hypersonic combustion. Advances in ignition systems target reliable and repeatable detonation initiation with minimal additional energy input or system complexity. Active flow control (AFC) techniques have also been investigated to manage unsteady interactions between shocks, reaction zones, and boundary layers. While these developments do not constitute new engine cycles, they go a long way towards practical detonation combustion cycles.
Taken together, these emerging and hybrid approaches demonstrate incremental progress towards practical pressure-gain combustion in airbreathing propulsion systems. However, they do not circumvent the fundamental limitations imposed by finite-time mixing, total pressure losses, and extreme environments. Instead, they redistribute these challenges in ways that improve operability and extend the feasible flight envelope.
